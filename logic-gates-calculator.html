The Power of Bitwise Operations in Programming

Bitwise operations are fundamental to low-level programming, used for tasks like managing flags in memory, encryption, network packet processing, and image manipulation. Unlike standard arithmetic operations, which treat numbers as a single value, bitwise operations treat them as a sequence of individual binary digits (bits).

Understanding Bitwise Logic

When you perform a **Bitwise AND** on two decimal numbers, you are essentially AND-ing the corresponding bits of their binary representations. For instance, $5 (00000101_2)$ AND $3 (00000011_2)$ results in $1 (00000001_2)$. This is often used to mask (clear) specific bits.

The Bitwise NOT Operator

The **Bitwise NOT** operation, or one's complement, flips every single bit (0 becomes 1, and 1 becomes 0). For instance, the Bitwise NOT of $5 (00000101_2)$ results in $250 (11111010_2)$ when constrained to an 8-bit system. This operation is crucial in digital math for implementing two's complement and negation.
